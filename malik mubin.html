لَا إِلَٰهَ إِلَّا اللَّهُ الْمَلِكُ الْحَقُّ الْمُبِينُ

Lā ilāha illallāh al-Malik al-Ḥaqq al-Mubīn — Muḥammad Rasūlullāh Ṣādiq al-Waʿd al-Amīn

📖 Context of the Dhikr

This noble invocation combines the pure declaration of Allāh’s absolute sovereignty and undeniable truth with the attestation of Prophet Muḥammad’s (ﷺ) truthfulness in promise and trustworthiness. It has been transmitted in several hadith works with varying degrees of authenticity, yet numerous scholars have affirmed its spiritual virtue as part of righteous deeds (faḍāʾil al-aʿmāl). The first part appears in Tārīkh Baghdād of al-Khaṭīb al-Baghdādī and Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ of Abū Nuʿaym, while the second half is widely known as the inscription on the door of the Kaʿbah and the Prophet’s Mosque [citation:1][citation:3][citation:8].

📜 The Narration Chain & Scholarly Notes

Al-Khaṭīb al-Baghdādī narrated via a chain —
“Whoever says every day one hundred times: Lā ilāha illallāh al-Malik al-Ḥaqq al-Mubīn, it will be for him a protection from poverty, he will acquire prosperity thereby, be safe from the loneliness of the grave, and by this kalimah he will knock at the door of Paradise.”

“The Prophet (ﷺ) said: ‘Whoever says each day one hundred times: There is no deity but Allah, the King, the Truth, the Clear — it becomes for him a protection from poverty, he acquires prosperity through it, he is safe from the loneliness of the grave, and by it he knocks at the door of Paradise.’”
Al-Khaṭīb al-Baghdādī (Abū Bakr Aḥmad ibn ʿAlī)

Full name: Abū Bakr Aḥmad ibn ʿAlī ibn Thābit ibn Aḥmad ibn Mahdī al-Baghdādī (392–463 AH / 1002–1071 CE). Eminent ḥadīth master, historian, and jurist of the Shāfiʿī school. Author of the monumental Tārīkh Baghdād (History of Baghdad), a 14-volume encyclopedia of narrators and scholars.

Known for his meticulous biographical methodology and critical scrutiny of chains. Al-Khaṭīb recorded this narration from chains through Imam Mālik, though later ḥadīth critics (al-Dāraquṭnī, Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr, al-Dhahabī) indicated weakness (ḍaʿīf) in the attribution to Mālik. However, the text remains a recognized supplication for virtues.

Role here: Primary compiler and transmitter of this hadith text in Tārīkh Baghdād (vol. 14, pg. 322), preserving it for scholarly transmission despite its chain vulnerabilities. The narration is cited within the framework of encouragement for good deeds (faḍāʾil al-aʿmāl) where leniency is traditionally practiced.

Part 1 · The Sovereignty & Clarity of Allāh
لَا no lā
إِلَٰهَ deity ilāha
إِلَّا except illā
ٱللَّٰهُ Allah allāh
ٱلْمَلِكُ the King al-malik
ٱلْحَقُّ the Truth al-ḥaqq
ٱلْمُبِينُ the Clear al-mubīn
“There is no deity worthy of worship except Allah, the Sovereign King, the Absolute Truth, the One who makes all things clear.”
Ḍaʿīf li-ʿillah but Ṣāliḥ li-l-faḍāʾil Recorded by al-Khaṭīb al-Baghdādī in Tārīkh Baghdād (14/322); also in Abū Nuʿaym’s Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ (8/309). Graded weak by al-Albānī, al-Dhahabī, and al-Dāraquṭnī due to the narrator al-Faḍl ibn Ghānim. Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr stated: “It is a good supplication and its blessing is hoped for.” [citation:1][citation:2][citation:4]
Part 2 · Attestation of the Prophet’s Truthfulness
مُحَمَّدٌ Muhammad muḥammad
رَسُولُ messenger rasūl
ٱللَّٰهِ of Allah allāh
صَادِقُ truthful ṣādiq
ٱلْوَعْدِ in promise al-waʿd
ٱلْأَمِينُ the trustworthy al-amīn
“Muḥammad is the Messenger of Allah, truthful in His promise, and the trustworthy one.”
Widely Known & Established This second phrase is not part of the primary narrated hadith text found in al-Khaṭīb’s report, but is an established part of the complete dhikr inscribed on the door of the Kaʿbah and the Prophet’s Mosque in Madīnah. It is affirmed by numerous scholars as a correct description of the Prophet ﷺ based on the Qurʾān (Sūrat al-Najm 53:3–4) and ḥadīth (Bukhārī, 3629; Muslim, 2320a). [citation:1][citation:6][citation:8]
✅ Source & Authenticity Assessment

The first half “Lā ilāha illallāh al-Malik al-Ḥaqq al-Mubīn” is documented in Tārīkh Baghdād (vol. 14, pg. 322) and Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ (vol. 8, pg. 309) with a chain containing weakness (ḍaʿīf). Prominent critics including Imām al-Dāraquṭnī, Ḥāfiẓ al-Dhahabī, and Shaykh al-Albānī classified the attribution to Mālik as unreliable [citation:2][citation:4][citation:5]. However, as a supplication for virtue (faḍāʾil al-aʿmāl), scholars like Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr permitted acting upon it, saying: “It is a good duʿāʾ and its blessing is hoped for” [citation:2][citation:4]. The second half “Muḥammad Rasūlullāh Ṣādiq al-Waʿd al-Amīn” carries authentic meanings confirmed by the Qurʾān and Ṣaḥīḥ ḥadīth, describing the Prophet’s perfect truthfulness and trustworthiness. The complete dhikr is widely recommended for daily recitation, particularly 100 times, for expansion of provision (rizq) and spiritual protection [citation:6].
